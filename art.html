The Adventures of Art, Son of Conn


    Idiots Guide to “The Adventures of Art, Son of Conn”

    by Rachel Tucker


The “Adventures of Art Son of Conn” can seem fairly confusing at first glance, with all the foreign names and places, but with a little context, the story seems to fit into a normal Irish kingship story. The story begins by introducing our main hero Art, telling us all of his ancestors. This is very common in Irish text as Irish people are named after their ancestors to give their lineage credibility. Like most Irish kingship stories, the first person we are truly introduced to in depth is a parent of the main hero, that parent being Conn King of Tara, or Ireland.

Conn becomes an unfit king when he marries the trickster Becuma Cneisgel, banishing his son unjustly by her command. In Ireland, Kingship requires the king have fir flathemon, which means the “truth of a prince”. If you make unjust decisions, you become unfit to rule and Ireland loses its prosperity. This is also shown when Conn’s new wife pushes for him to murder the sinless boy, Segda Saerlabraid, to bring back prosperity to Ireland. Really, if he divorced her, prosperity would return to his rule. Luckily, Segda’s mother was there to save him, and Conn realized Becuma was dangerous, however he could not divorce her. But somehow, they would have to find a way of ridding Tara of her siege.

We then begin the competition between Art and Becuma, who are now enemies. Becuma, who we can tell is otherworldly, attempts to put a geis on Art, or a rule that cannot be broken without dire consequences, unless he plays a game of chess with her. He plays and wins, and so starts the competition of who can get rid of who. Because he won, Art puts a geis on Becuma that she may not eat while in the lands of Tara until she retrieves Cu Roi mac Dairi’s wand for him. She starts her search by visiting Tara’s sid, or fairy mounds which are entrances to the other world, and eventually meets a foster sister at one. The foster sister gives her directions on how to find the wand, and with some difficulty she eventually completes her geis. When Becuma returns to Tara, she places a geis upon Art as penance, sending him off to the other world to find Delbchaem daughter of Morgan and bring her back to Tara. Delbchaem could only be found in the land of Wonders, which meant Art had to leave Tara and venture to the otherworld to find this mysterious woman.

This part of the story begins to become a wooing tale, known in Irish as a Tochmarc. A Tochmarc is a tale in which a young king or hero seeks out a woman he has only heard of to make her his wife. Art begins his journey by going though a series of ordeals involving poisonous frogs, “perverse hags”, and few other nature events until he comes to his first battle. He fights two men to gain information on Delbchaem’s whereabouts, and eventually finds her. The only problem is, her parents are unwilling to part with her. This is a common motif in Tochmarcs, where parents give suitors impossible tasks to try and dissuaded the man from pursuing the woman. In this story, Delbchaem’s mother, Coinchenn Cennfada, is a warrior woman and challenges Art to fight her for her daughter. Art obliges, and kills Coinchenn, which causes Morgan, Delbchaem’s father, to avenge his wife, and eventually be killed by Art too. Art then takes a wooed Debchaem, who was finally free of her overbearing parents, back to Tara. With this feat completed, Art banishes Becuma from Tara, and everyone lived happily ever after.
